Supreme Court Criticizes NMC's 'Both Hands Intact' Rule for MBBS Admission

In a significant judgment, the Supreme Court of India has strongly criticized the National Medical Commission's (NMC) requirement that MBBS aspirants must have "both hands intact" to pursue medical studies. The court deemed this rule discriminatory and a manifestation of "ableism," which unfairly favors individuals with typical physical abilities over those with disabilities.

Background of the Case

The case involved a candidate with a locomotor disability of 50% and a speech and language disability of 20%, totaling a final disability computation of 58%. Despite clearing the National Eligibility cum Entrance Test (NEET) 2024, the candidate was denied admission due to the NMC's eligibility criteria. The Supreme Court intervened after the Punjab and Haryana High Court refused to override the expert opinion that deemed the candidate ineligible.

Supreme Court's Observations

The bench, comprising Justices B.R. Gavai and K.V. Viswanathan, observed that the "both hands intact" requirement is "completely antithetical" to Article 41 of the Constitution, the principles of the United Nations Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities, and the Rights of Persons with Disabilities Act (RPwD Act, 2016). The court noted that this rule promotes an overbroad classification that glorifies ableism, undermining the rights guaranteed by the Constitution and the RPwD Act.

The court emphasized that such a prescription has no legal sanctity because it fails to consider individual functional assessments, which are crucial for protecting the rights of persons with disabilities. It highlighted that the approach should focus on providing reasonable accommodations and assistive technologies rather than presuming incompetence at the outset.

Dr. Satendra Singh's Assessment

The court praised the approach of Dr. Satendra Singh, who suggested that candidates with disabilities should not be barred at the threshold. Instead, they should be allowed to complete the MBBS course and later decide whether to specialize in non-surgical or medical branches or continue as general duty medical officers. Dr. Singh's report emphasized the potential for candidates to successfully navigate the MBBS course with appropriate clinical accommodations and assistive technologies.

Court's Directive to NMC

The Supreme Court directed the NMC to revise its guidelines and report progress by March 2025. This directive follows the court's previous order in the Omkar Ramchandra Gond case, where it had asked the NMC to issue revised regulations for the admission of students with specified disabilities under the RPwD Act.

Implications

The judgment marks a significant step towards inclusivity in medical education, emphasizing the need for a more nuanced approach that considers individual capabilities rather than blanket criteria. It underscores the importance of reasonable accommodation and assistive technologies in enabling persons with disabilities to pursue careers in medicine.

In conclusion, the Supreme Court's ruling challenges the NMC to adopt a more inclusive and flexible approach to medical admissions, aligning with constitutional principles and international human rights standards. This decision is expected to pave the way for more equitable opportunities for individuals with disabilities in the medical field.
